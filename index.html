The problem

Most websites weren't built with everyone in mind.

Tiny click targets. Gray text on gray backgrounds. Dense pages with nowhere for your eyes to rest. Expensive overlays that hide behind a paywall. Most accessibility products assume you will change how you work to fit the tool.

We think it should be the opposite. Software should meet people where they already are: in the browser, on the sites they use every day.

1 in 4

US adults has a disability that can affect how they use the web.

CDC disability prevalence data

  • A parent can't read the fine print Medical forms, school notices, and billing pages in type too small to parse.
  • A student gives up on the portal Too many menus, too little contrast, and no way to make the page stay readable.
  • Hands too tired to keep clicking Banking, shopping, and email still demand perfect aim on tiny buttons.

The gap we close

Practical controls on the sites people already visit. No platform switch. No six-figure overlay contract.

Customer stories

Every control starts with a customer need.

We do not lead with feature lists. We start with the challenge someone is living through, the support they need, and the independence they gain.

For students overwhelmed by cluttered school portals

Challenge
Schedules, assignments, and announcements buried in dense layouts and tiny type.
Solution
Accessibility Surfer enlarges text, opens breathing room between lines, and strips a page down to the article you actually need.
Result
Finish homework and find deadlines without asking someone else to click through for you.

For parents struggling to read small text online

Challenge
Permission slips, pharmacy instructions, and account pages that assume perfect vision.
Solution
Stronger contrast, larger type, and dyslexia-friendly fonts on any website you already use.
Result
Read forms and emails on your own, at your pace, without zooming and losing the page layout.

For anyone whose hands are too tired to keep clicking

Challenge
Tremors, pain, fatigue, or a wrist in a cast. Every button press costs something.
Solution
Hover-to-click on links, buttons, and video thumbnails. Point, wait, and let the browser act for you.
Result
Shop, bank, and browse with less strain and fewer missed targets.

For people navigating with eye gaze or head tracking

Challenge
Moving a cursor is possible. Pressing a button reliably often is not.
Solution
Dwell clicking that works on every site, with separate timing for video players and regular pages.
Result
More independence online without waiting for a caregiver to click for you.

For readers who lose focus on noisy pages

Challenge
Ads, sidebars, and pop-ups pull attention away from the one paragraph you came for.
Solution
Reading mode removes the clutter and lets you move through an article one block at a time.
Result
Finish the story, the policy, or the tutorial without giving up halfway.

For site owners who want visitors to feel welcome

Challenge
Visitors leave because the site is hard to read, not because they do not care about your content.
Solution
AccessFlow adds a visitor-controlled toolbar in one line of code. No rebuild, no enterprise contract.
Result
Every visitor can adapt your site to how they read and navigate, and you stay focused on your product.

Why Axol Assist exists

Software that adapts to people.

Accessibility tools should be practical, not performative. Too many solutions are expensive, buried in settings, or hidden behind overlays that site owners never enable. Real users are left to fight the default web on their own.

Axol Assist exists to build software that meets people where they are. We ship browser tools for individuals and embeddable tools for site owners. We test with real users, keep data in the browser, and charge fairly so more people can actually use what we make.

Our commitment: build accessibility technology that is usable on day one, respectful of privacy, and honest about what it can and cannot fix.
